What Are Security Operations?

Security operations refers to the ongoing organizational effort to monitor, detect, analyze, and respond to cybersecurity threats. It sits at the intersection of people, technology, and process, combining trained analysts, detection tooling, and documented procedures into a system designed to catch and contain attacks before they cause significant harm.

Unlike compliance-oriented security work, which focuses on meeting a standard at a point in time, security operations is a continuous function. Adversaries do not observe business hours, and many intrusions are designed to move slowly and quietly over days or weeks. An effective security operations program keeps eyes on the environment at all times, looking for anomalies, validating alerts, and connecting small signals into a coherent picture of what is happening across the network.

The scope of security operations has expanded significantly as organizations have moved workloads to the cloud, adopted remote work at scale, and accumulated larger and more fragmented technology estates. A modern SecOps program has to account for endpoints, cloud infrastructure, identity systems, network traffic, third-party integrations, and more. That breadth makes both the job harder and the investment more consequential.

The Modern Security Operations Workflow

Security operations functions as a continuous cycle rather than a series of isolated tasks. When a potential threat surfaces, it moves through a defined operational lifecycle. Understanding that lifecycle helps both practitioners and organizational leaders evaluate how well a program is actually functioning.

Modern security teams operate across all hours of the day, often using shift rotations or follow-the-sun models to ensure no period of low visibility. Large enterprises may staff a fully dedicated SOC. Smaller organizations increasingly use Managed Detection and Response (MDR) providers to extend coverage without the overhead of building an in-house team from scratch.

The Detection-to-Recovery Cycle

Phase What Happens Key Output
Detection Security tools generate alerts from logs, endpoint telemetry, and network data based on detection rules and behavioral analytics Alert queue requiring analyst review
Investigation Analysts pull additional context to determine whether an alert represents a real threat or a false positive Confirmed incident or closed alert
Containment Confirmed incidents are isolated to limit damage — isolating endpoints, blocking domains, disabling compromised accounts Attacker lateral movement halted
Remediation Malware removed, exploited vulnerabilities patched, compromised credentials reset, persistence mechanisms eliminated Attacker presence eliminated
Recovery Affected systems restored to normal operation, environment verified clean, monitoring heightened for re-compromise Normal operations resumed
Lessons Learned Post-incident review documents what happened, what worked, and what should improve — feeding back into detection rules and response procedures Program improvements implemented

Security Operations Centers (SOC)

The Security Operations Center is the organizational hub of a security operations program. It is the team — and often the physical or virtual space — where analysts monitor telemetry, triage alerts, investigate suspicious activity, and coordinate response efforts. For many organizations, the SOC represents the front line of cyber defense.

SOCs are typically structured around a tiered analyst model that distributes work based on complexity and required expertise. This structure allows high-volume, lower-complexity alert triage to be handled at scale while reserving senior analyst time for sophisticated threat investigations and threat hunting activity.

The Tiered Analyst Model

Tier Role Primary Responsibilities
Tier 1 Alert Triage Analyst Monitor security dashboards, review and categorize incoming alerts, follow defined playbooks, escalate confirmed or suspicious activity. High alert volume, structured workflow.
Tier 2 Incident Analyst Deep-dive investigation of escalated events, correlating log data across multiple sources, determining scope of compromise, coordinating containment actions.
Tier 3 Senior Analyst / Threat Hunter Proactive threat hunting, advanced forensics, malware analysis, threat intelligence integration, detection rule development, and mentoring lower tiers.

SOC Technology Stack

Beyond analyst staffing, a SOC depends on a well-integrated technology stack. At the center is typically a Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) platform that aggregates logs from across the environment and provides a unified interface for searching, correlating, and alerting on security events. Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR) tools provide granular visibility into activity on individual endpoints. Network monitoring, cloud security posture management tools, and identity integrations round out the full picture.

The effectiveness of a SOC is not measured purely by headcount or tool investment. How analysts use those tools, the quality of detection rules, the clarity of escalation processes, and the feedback loops between incident response and detection improvement all determine whether a SOC is a genuine defensive capability or an expensive set of dashboards that nobody trusts.

What Makes a SOC Effective
  • Alert quality: High-fidelity detection rules that surface real threats without generating overwhelming noise that conditions analysts to ignore alerts
  • Analyst training: Consistent investment in skill development across all tiers, particularly in threat intelligence application and investigation methodology
  • Documented playbooks: Defined response procedures for common incident types so analysts do not improvise under pressure
  • Tool integration: SIEM, EDR, and SOAR platforms that share data and enable analysts to act quickly across the full environment
  • Metrics and feedback: Tracking mean time to detect and respond, false positive rates, and escalation accuracy to identify where the program needs improvement
  • Threat intelligence context: Intelligence feeds and analyst research that give alerts meaning beyond raw log data
01
Alert Fires

At 2:17 AM, the SIEM fires a medium-severity alert: a Windows endpoint has executed PowerShell with an encoded command and established an outbound connection to an external IP. The Tier 1 analyst on shift sees the alert in the queue.

02
Initial Triage

The Tier 1 analyst checks the host against the asset inventory. It is a workstation belonging to an accountant who has no legitimate reason to run encoded PowerShell. The external IP resolves to a hosting provider with no business relationship. The alert is escalated to Tier 2 immediately.

03
Investigation

The Tier 2 analyst pulls EDR telemetry for the host. The encoded PowerShell decoded to a command that downloaded and executed a second-stage payload from the same external IP. Process genealogy shows the parent process was a Microsoft Office document opened via a phishing email three hours earlier. The incident is confirmed.

04
Containment

The Tier 2 analyst isolates the endpoint via EDR, disables the user account pending investigation, and blocks the external IP and associated domain at the perimeter. The incident is handed to the Tier 3 analyst and incident response team for full investigation and scope determination.

ATT&CK
T1566.001 — Spearphishing Attachment → T1059.001 — PowerShell → T1071.001 — Web Protocols C2

The scenario above maps to a classic initial access chain. The phishing document (T1566.001) drops an Office macro that spawns encoded PowerShell (T1059.001), which beacons over HTTPS to attacker infrastructure (T1071.001). SOC analysts who know this kill chain can validate or rule out each stage quickly rather than investigating each alert in isolation.

Cyber Threat Intelligence

Threat intelligence is the discipline of researching and understanding the adversaries, campaigns, and techniques that pose a risk to an organization — and converting that knowledge into something operationally useful for defenders. Good intelligence answers questions that raw data cannot: who is targeting organizations like ours, what tools and techniques are they using, and where should we focus our detection efforts right now? A mature threat intelligence program shifts defenders from purely reactive postures to anticipatory ones.

The threat intelligence lifecycle describes how raw information becomes usable intelligence. It begins with defining requirements — what questions does the security team need answered? Intelligence is then collected from technical sources like malware repositories, dark web forums, open-source feeds, and information sharing communities. Raw data is processed, analyzed, and assessed for reliability before being disseminated to the people and systems that need it.

Intelligence Types

Intelligence exists at multiple levels of abstraction, and different stakeholders need different types:

The Four Intelligence Types
  • Strategic Intelligence: High-level analysis of threat actors, geopolitical factors, and industry trends. Useful for executive decision-makers and security program investment decisions. Long time horizon, non-technical.
  • Operational Intelligence: Campaign-level intelligence about specific threat actors, their targeting preferences, and the infrastructure they use. Helps defenders understand active threats relevant to their sector.
  • Tactical Intelligence: Detailed adversary techniques, tools, and procedures mapped to frameworks like MITRE ATT&CK. Directly informs detection engineering and analyst investigation workflows.
  • Technical Intelligence: Specific indicators of compromise — IP addresses, domains, file hashes, URLs — that can be ingested into security tools for automated detection and blocking.

How Intelligence Improves Security Operations

The most important contribution intelligence makes to security operations is context. When a SOC analyst sees an alert, intelligence can tell them whether the observed technique is consistent with a known threat actor targeting their industry, whether the indicators have been seen in active campaigns, and what the next likely attacker moves might be. That context converts an isolated alert into a meaningful threat narrative, which improves triage accuracy and speeds up investigation.

Organizations that build mature threat intelligence programs shift from purely reactive postures toward anticipatory defense — identifying likely attack patterns before they materialize and building detection coverage against them in advance. The MITRE ATT&CK framework provides a common language for describing adversary techniques, making it easier to map intelligence findings to detection gaps.

Incident Response

Incident response (IR) is the structured process an organization follows when a security incident is confirmed. The goal is to limit damage, reduce recovery time, and gather enough information to prevent recurrence. Without a defined incident response process, organizations improvise under pressure — which typically leads to slower response, incomplete remediation, and recurring incidents.

The six-phase incident response lifecycle is the most widely referenced framework in the field. Each phase builds on the previous one, and the lessons learned phase feeds directly back into improving preparation for future incidents.

The Six-Phase IR Lifecycle

Phase 1 — Preparation

Before an incident occurs, organizations must build the capability to respond effectively. This means defining response roles and responsibilities, documenting playbooks for common incident types, establishing communication protocols, ensuring IR tools are deployed and accessible, and conducting tabletop exercises to test readiness. A team that has never rehearsed a ransomware response will perform far worse during a real event than one that has walked through it multiple times.

Phase 2 — Identification

This phase involves determining that a security incident has actually occurred, establishing its initial scope, and classifying its severity. Identification draws on alerting data, analyst investigation, threat hunting findings, and sometimes external notification from law enforcement or third parties. The sooner a real incident is identified and separated from false positives, the sooner effective response can begin.

Phase 3 — Containment

Containment halts the spread of an incident while preserving evidence for investigation. Short-term containment actions — network isolation, account suspension, traffic blocking — happen fast to limit immediate damage. Longer-term containment involves more deliberate measures that allow business operations to continue while the full scope of the incident is being determined.

Phase 4 — Eradication

With the incident contained, the team removes the attacker's presence from the environment. This includes identifying and deleting malware, removing attacker-created accounts, closing exploited vulnerabilities, and eliminating any persistence mechanisms — scheduled tasks, registry keys, or backdoors — that would allow the attacker to regain access. Eradication must be thorough; partial cleanup often leads to re-compromise.

Phase 5 — Recovery

Systems are restored to normal operation after eradication is confirmed. Recovery involves rebuilding or restoring affected systems, validating that they are functioning correctly, verifying that no residual attacker access exists, and returning business processes to normal. Monitoring is heightened during the recovery window to catch any signs of re-compromise.

Phase 6 — Lessons Learned

A post-incident review documents what happened, how it was handled, what worked, and what should improve. Lessons learned feed directly into all other phases — updating detection rules, improving playbooks, identifying training gaps, and initiating remediation work on vulnerabilities identified during the investigation. Without this phase, the same incident patterns tend to repeat.

Vulnerability Management

Vulnerability management is the continuous process of finding, assessing, prioritizing, and remediating security weaknesses across an organization's technology environment. It is fundamentally a risk reduction discipline: the goal is not to eliminate every known vulnerability — that is impossible at any meaningful scale — but to close the most dangerous gaps before attackers find and exploit them. A well-run vulnerability management program is one of the highest-leverage investments a security team can make.

Effective vulnerability management requires a clear view of what assets exist in the environment, what vulnerabilities affect those assets, which of those vulnerabilities represent meaningful risk given the organization's threat landscape, and what the remediation or mitigation path looks like for each.

The Vulnerability Management Lifecycle

Stage Activity Key Consideration
Asset Discovery Build and maintain an accurate inventory of all systems, applications, and services — including cloud resources and shadow IT You cannot protect what you do not know exists
Vulnerability Scanning Automated tools query systems for known vulnerabilities based on version analysis and comparison against CVE databases Scanning frequency must keep pace with newly disclosed vulnerabilities
Risk Prioritization CVSS scores combined with exploit availability, asset criticality, and threat intelligence context to focus remediation effort Known exploited vulnerabilities in active campaigns take highest priority
Patch Management Updates tested, approved, and deployed within timeframes commensurate with the severity and exploitability of each vulnerability Patching timelines should be risk-tiered, not uniformly scheduled
Continuous Remediation New vulnerabilities discovered continuously as software changes and new CVEs publish — remediation is an ongoing operation, not a periodic project Vulnerability debt compounds quickly without a continuous process

Threat Intelligence Integration

Vulnerability management programs that incorporate threat intelligence can dramatically improve prioritization accuracy. Knowing that a specific vulnerability is being actively exploited by a threat actor targeting your industry — as tracked in sources like CISA's Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog — elevates its urgency far beyond what CVSS score alone would suggest. The intersection of high CVSS score, public exploit availability, and confirmed active exploitation represents the highest-priority remediation targets in any environment.

01
Scan Results

A vulnerability scan returns 847 findings across production systems. Sorting by CVSS score yields 23 Critical and 94 High severity findings. The security team has capacity to remediate roughly 30 vulnerabilities this cycle. Applying CVSS rank alone does not meaningfully narrow the list given how many share identical scores.

02
Intelligence Overlay

The team cross-references the Critical findings against the CISA KEV catalog and a threat intelligence feed tracking active campaigns against their industry vertical. Three of the 23 Critical vulnerabilities appear in active exploitation campaigns by threat actors known to target manufacturing sector organizations. Those three jump to the top of the list regardless of their relative CVSS scores.

03
Asset Criticality

Of the remaining 20 Critical findings, the team filters by asset criticality. Vulnerabilities affecting internet-facing systems or those hosting sensitive production data are prioritized over internal development systems with limited network exposure. This brings the prioritized list to a manageable 28 findings for the current remediation cycle.

04
Outcome

The three actively-exploited vulnerabilities are patched within 48 hours. The remaining 25 prioritized findings are remediated across the two-week sprint window. The team's effort is concentrated where actual risk lives rather than distributed evenly across a raw CVSS-sorted list that does not reflect real-world threat context.

Detection Engineering

Detection engineering is the practice of systematically building, testing, and maintaining the detection logic that makes security monitoring actually work. It bridges the gap between knowing what adversaries do — through threat intelligence and purple team exercises — and having the technical rules and analytics in place to surface that activity reliably from logs and telemetry. It is one of the fastest-growing specializations in the field, and for good reason: without it, even the most expensive SIEM becomes a noise machine that defenders learn to ignore.

The discipline has grown in prominence as organizations have recognized that out-of-the-box detection rules from SIEM vendors are rarely sufficient on their own. Default rules tend to generate high false positive volumes, miss environment-specific attacker patterns, and fail to keep pace with evolving adversary techniques. Detection engineers address those gaps through a continuous development cycle that treats detection content as code.

Core Detection Engineering Practices

Detection Rule Development

Writing detection logic in query languages like SIGMA, KQL, SPL, or platform-specific formats. Rules are built from threat intelligence, ATT&CK technique analysis, and observed attacker behavior in the specific environment. Effective rules balance specificity — catching real threats — against generality — catching variations of known techniques.

ATT&CK
T1059 — Command and Scripting Interpreter

One of the most frequently detected technique categories. Adversaries abuse scripting environments — PowerShell (T1059.001), Bash (T1059.004), Python (T1059.006) — to execute payloads, move laterally, and establish persistence. High-fidelity detections for this technique family are foundational to any detection library.

Log Coverage Assessment

Evaluating what data sources are available, whether they are properly ingested, and whether the right telemetry exists to detect specific attack techniques. Detection gaps are as important to document as detection rules themselves. A gap map against the MITRE ATT&CK framework gives programs a structured view of where visibility is weakest.

ATT&CK
T1078 — Valid Accounts & T1550 — Use Alternate Authentication Material

Identity-based attacks are consistently among the hardest to detect due to log coverage gaps. Detecting T1078 (credential abuse) and T1550.002 (Pass the Hash) requires Windows Security Event Log, Sysmon, and identity provider logs to all be ingested and correlated — a common gap in coverage assessments.

Tuning and False Positive Reduction

A detection rule that fires constantly on benign activity trains analysts to ignore it. Tuning is the ongoing process of refining logic to reduce noise while maintaining coverage — one of the most time-intensive parts of detection engineering work, requiring both technical understanding of normal environment behavior and analytical judgment about acceptable risk tolerance.

Detection-as-Code

Treating detection content with the same rigor as software: version control, peer review, automated testing, and deployment pipelines that validate rules before they reach production. This approach improves quality, enables rapid iteration, and ensures that changes to detection content are tracked and reversible. Teams using detection-as-code practices consistently produce higher-quality detection libraries than those managing rules ad hoc.

01
Intelligence Input

A threat intelligence report identifies that a threat actor actively targeting the organization's sector is using encoded PowerShell commands to download second-stage payloads — consistent with ATT&CK T1059.001. The detection engineer reviews the TTP details and pulls the relevant SIGMA rules community as a starting reference.

02
Coverage Gap Assessment

The engineer queries the SIEM for PowerShell execution events over the last 30 days. The telemetry exists but there is no active rule targeting encoded command-line arguments (-EncodedCommand or -enc flags) combined with outbound network connections. The gap is documented in the ATT&CK coverage matrix against T1059.001.

03
Rule Development & Testing

A KQL detection rule is written targeting: process name powershell.exe, command line containing "-enc" or "-EncodedCommand", and a child network connection within 60 seconds. The red team runs the technique in a sandbox and confirms the rule fires. The rule is tuned against 30 days of historical data — two false positives are identified and allowlisted (a legitimate IT automation script).

04
Deployment & Documentation

The rule is committed to the detection-as-code repository with a PR review, linked to the ATT&CK technique ID, intelligence source, and tuning notes. It deploys to production SIEM via CI/CD pipeline. The ATT&CK coverage matrix is updated — T1059.001 moves from "gap" to "detected." The SOC team is notified of the new rule and provided investigation guidance for when it fires.

Purple Team Integration

The most mature detection engineering programs run continuous purple team exercises that simulate adversary techniques in the environment, validate whether existing detection rules fire, and identify gaps that need new rule development. This creates a feedback loop that directly ties adversary behavior to detection coverage. Intelligence about a new campaign targeting your sector can be operationalized as both a detection rule and a purple team test scenario within the same sprint cycle.

How These Functions Work Together

The five security operations disciplines are more valuable in combination than any of them is in isolation. An organization with excellent threat intelligence but no detection engineering to operationalize it is sitting on a library nobody reads. A SOC with capable analysts but no intelligence context is triaging alerts in the dark. Incident response without strong detection coverage means incidents get identified late, when damage is already done.

The most effective security programs treat these functions as an integrated system with continuous feedback flowing between them. The following walkthrough illustrates how a threat can be addressed end-to-end when the functions are properly connected.

This model is sometimes described as threat-informed defense — where knowledge of real adversary behavior directly shapes detection priorities, defensive investments, and response procedures. Frameworks like MITRE ATT&CK exist specifically to support this kind of structured alignment between threat knowledge and defensive action.

An End-to-End Integrated Response

Step Function What Happens
1 Threat Intelligence Analysts identify a campaign targeting organizations in your sector. Initial access techniques, post-compromise tools, and indicators of compromise are documented and shared with the detection engineering and SOC teams.
2 Detection Engineering Engineers map campaign techniques to ATT&CK, evaluate whether existing rules would catch the observed behaviors, and develop new detection logic for gaps. Rules are tested and pushed to the SIEM ahead of the anticipated campaign targeting window.
3 Vulnerability Management The campaign exploits a specific vulnerability for initial access. The VM team identifies affected systems, prioritizes the patch above normal scheduling, and verifies remediation across the environment — closing the door before the attack arrives.
4 SOC Informed by intelligence context, analysts are primed to watch for specific activity patterns. When a new rule fires matching expected post-exploitation behavior, analysts have the context they need to quickly confirm a real intrusion and escalate appropriately.
5 Incident Response A confirmed intrusion is handed to IR. Because detection caught the activity early in the attack chain, scope is limited. The team contains and eradicates the threat, and feeds new intelligence and detection improvements back into the cycle through lessons learned.
ATT&CK
TA0001 → TA0002 → TA0003 — Initial Access, Execution, Persistence

The integrated response model maps directly to ATT&CK tactics. Threat intelligence identifies adversary preference for specific Initial Access (TA0001) techniques. Detection engineering builds coverage for the expected Execution (TA0002) behaviors. Vulnerability management closes the doors the attacker needs for initial entry. When detection fires, SOC and IR work the Persistence (TA0003) and lateral movement tactics that follow. ATT&CK provides the common language that makes this coordination possible.

Security Operations Technology Architecture

Modern security operations programs are supported by a layered technology stack. No single tool provides complete visibility or control across the entire environment, so organizations deploy multiple specialized platforms that each contribute a different data type or capability. The following technologies form the core of most mature security operations architectures.

Log Aggregation & Alerting
SIEM

Aggregates log and event data from across the environment, enables search, correlation, and alerting. Central console for most SOC workflows. Examples: Splunk, Microsoft Sentinel, Elastic SIEM, IBM QRadar.

Endpoint Visibility
EDR

Deep visibility into endpoint activity — process execution, file changes, registry modifications, network connections. Enables remote isolation and investigation. Examples: CrowdStrike Falcon, Microsoft Defender for Endpoint, SentinelOne.

Workflow Automation
SOAR

Automates repetitive analyst tasks — alert enrichment, indicator lookups, ticket creation, containment actions — and orchestrates workflows across multiple security tools to reduce workload and accelerate response times.

Intelligence Management
TIP

Aggregates intelligence feeds, manages indicator lifecycle, enables analyst research, and integrates with SIEM and other tools to operationalize intelligence. Examples: MISP, Recorded Future, ThreatConnect, OpenCTI.

Network Visibility
NDR

Monitors network traffic for anomalous behavior and known attack patterns. Particularly valuable for detecting lateral movement and data exfiltration that may not surface through endpoint-only visibility.

Cloud Security
CSPM / CNAPP

Extends security operations visibility into cloud infrastructure, identifying misconfigurations, excessive permissions, and cloud-specific attack techniques across multi-cloud environments.

Integration between these platforms is as important as the platforms themselves. A SIEM that cannot ingest EDR telemetry, or a SOAR that cannot automate actions in the EDR, limits the speed and coverage of the overall capability. Mature programs invest significantly in integration engineering to ensure data flows freely between tools and that response actions can be executed quickly across the full environment.

Building an Effective Security Operations Program

Building a security operations program is a significant organizational investment that takes time to mature. Most programs do not start fully formed. They begin with the highest-priority capabilities, build competency, and expand scope over time. Understanding the key dimensions of program development helps organizations set realistic expectations and make sound investment decisions.

Staffing and Skills

Security operations is labor-intensive and requires specialized expertise. A capable SOC needs analysts who can work under pressure, think like adversaries, and communicate clearly. The chronic talent shortage in cybersecurity makes staffing difficult, and organizations need realistic plans for recruiting, developing internal talent, and retaining experienced people. For organizations that cannot build a fully staffed in-house team, managed service providers and MDR vendors offer a viable path to coverage without the full overhead of an internal program.

Tooling Selection

The technology choices made when standing up a security operations program shape its capabilities for years. Organizations should prioritize tools that address their specific threat landscape, integrate well with their existing environment, and are actually usable by the analysts they have. Expensive platforms with poor adoption due to complexity deliver less value than simpler tools that analysts use effectively every day. Proof-of-concept evaluations with actual SOC analysts are essential before major platform commitments.

Automation

Alert volumes in modern environments exceed what human analysts can manually review without automation support. SOAR platforms and SIEM-native automation enable programs to automatically enrich alerts with context, filter obvious false positives, execute first-response containment actions, and ensure consistent handling of routine event types. Automation does not replace analyst judgment — it removes the repetitive work that prevents analysts from focusing on the investigations that actually require human reasoning.

Threat Intelligence Integration

Programs that operate without threat intelligence context tend to focus exclusively on what is happening in their environment without adequate understanding of why. Integrating even basic open-source intelligence feeds into SIEM alerting and analyst workflows significantly improves the quality of detection and triage decisions. More mature programs build dedicated intelligence functions that produce tailored analysis for their specific sector and threat landscape.

Metrics and Continuous Improvement

Security operations programs should be measured against meaningful outcomes, not activity. Mean time to detect (MTTD) and mean time to respond (MTTR) are the most fundamental measures of SOC effectiveness. Detection coverage percentage against known adversary techniques, false positive rates, and vulnerability remediation velocity provide additional signal. These metrics should be reviewed regularly, with honest assessment of where the program is falling short and clear improvement plans to address identified gaps.

Security Operations Program Maturity Indicators
  • Initial: Ad hoc monitoring, no documented playbooks, reactive only — incidents discovered by users or external parties rather than internal detection
  • Developing: SIEM deployed and alerting, basic playbooks for common incident types, formal IR process in place, vulnerability scanning running on a schedule
  • Defined: Tiered SOC model operational, threat intelligence feeds integrated, detection rules mapped to ATT&CK, MTTD and MTTR tracked and improving
  • Managed: Detection-as-code practices in place, purple team exercises running regularly, vulnerability management risk-prioritized with intelligence context, automation reducing analyst workload
  • Optimizing: Threat-informed defense model fully operational, proactive threat hunting program, continuous improvement driven by metrics, intelligence program producing tailored sector-specific analysis
