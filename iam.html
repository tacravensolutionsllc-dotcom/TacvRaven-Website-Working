What is IAM?

Understanding Identity and Access Management and how organizations control user access to systems, applications, and data across modern enterprise environments.

In the modern enterprise, the network perimeter has dissolved. Users access systems from anywhere, on any device, through any cloud. The question is no longer whether someone can reach your network — it is whether they should. Identity and Access Management (IAM) is the discipline that answers that question. By governing who gets in, what they can do, and when that access ends, IAM has become the foundational security layer for organizations of every size and mission profile. Without a mature IAM posture, every other security investment is built on shifting ground.

Executive Overview

Identity and Access Management (IAM) is the discipline of systematically governing how digital identities are created, authenticated, authorized, and deprovisioned within an organization. An IAM system determines who a user is — and then enforces what that user is permitted to do. Every login, every API call, every access request passes through this framework, making IAM one of the most consequential security investments an organization can make across its entire technology portfolio.

IAM is critical to cybersecurity because unauthorized access is the root cause behind a majority of data breaches. Stolen credentials, excessive permissions, and unmanaged accounts are among the most frequently exploited attack vectors in modern incidents. IAM controls directly address these vulnerabilities by enforcing authentication standards, limiting access to the minimum required, and removing accounts that are no longer needed before they become available for attacker exploitation through credential theft or brute-force attack.

Historically, organizations relied on perimeter-based security: firewalls, VPNs, and network zones that separated “inside” from “outside.” Cloud adoption, remote work, and software-as-a-service have made this boundary largely meaningless. Analysts and practitioners have consequently declared that identity is the new perimeter. If you can answer — with confidence — who is accessing what resource at any given moment, you possess the most important visibility capability in modern security operations and incident response.

IAM systems enforce access control policies by combining identity verification with entitlement management. When a user requests access to a resource, the IAM system checks their verified identity against a policy engine that defines what that identity is allowed to do. The decision — grant or deny — happens in milliseconds, transparently, across every application and service enrolled in the IAM platform. That speed and consistency, applied across thousands of users and hundreds of applications simultaneously, is what makes IAM architecturally essential rather than merely operationally convenient for organizations managing access at enterprise scale.

Understanding Identity and Access Management

A digital identity is the collection of attributes that uniquely represent a user, system, or entity within a computing environment. For a human user, this includes their username, credentials, role assignments, group memberships, department, and other contextual data. For a machine or service, it might include a certificate, API key, or service account identifier. IAM manages all of these identity types across their entire lifecycle — from provisioning at onboarding to deprovisioning at separation.

IAM systems verify users by collecting evidence of identity — credentials, tokens, biometrics — and checking that evidence against authoritative identity stores such as LDAP directories, Active Directory, or cloud identity providers. Once identity is confirmed, the system consults its policy engine to determine what resources that identity is authorized to access and under what conditions. The distinction between two core IAM concepts is foundational to understanding how these systems function and where they can fail:

Authentication

Who are you? Authentication is the process of verifying that a user or system is who or what they claim to be. A password, a hardware token, a biometric scan — these are all authentication mechanisms. Authentication happens first and must succeed before any access decision is made. A failed authentication attempt produces no authorization evaluation at all, regardless of what entitlements the identity might otherwise hold.

Authorization

What are you allowed to do? Authorization happens after successful authentication. The system checks the authenticated identity’s entitlements — roles, group memberships, access policies — to determine which resources and operations are permitted. Passing authentication does not guarantee access; the authorization policy makes the final determination for each specific resource request independently from the authentication decision.

Identity Lifecycle

How is identity governed over time? Digital identities are not static. They are created when a user joins, modified as roles and responsibilities change, and must be promptly deprovisioned when a user departs. Orphaned accounts — active identities no longer belonging to an authorized, active user — are among the most consistently exploited attack surfaces in enterprise environments of every size, representing low-effort, low-detection initial access opportunities for adversaries conducting targeted intrusion campaigns.

Entitlement Management

What access exists across the organization? Entitlement management provides visibility into who has access to what across all connected systems. Regular access reviews and recertification campaigns identify and revoke excessive, outdated, or unauthorized entitlements — closing the gap between what access should exist based on current job function and what has accumulated through provisioning decisions that were never revisited or reversed across organizational transitions and role changes.

Understanding these distinctions is not academic — they directly inform how security teams design controls, investigate incidents, and respond to access anomalies. A compromised authentication credential is a fundamentally different problem with different remediation requirements than an over-permissioned authorization entitlement, and the IAM program must address both dimensions rigorously and independently to maintain a defensible access posture across the enterprise.

Core Components of IAM

A production-grade IAM deployment is not a single product — it is an integrated set of capabilities that together govern every aspect of digital identity in the organization. Understanding each component and how they interoperate is essential for security architects designing or maturing an IAM program that must operate reliably across a heterogeneous environment of on-premises systems, cloud platforms, and third-party applications.

Identity Lifecycle Management

Governs the creation, modification, and deletion of digital identities across their entire lifespan within the organization. This encompasses onboarding new employees and provisioning their access across relevant systems, updating roles and entitlements during organizational transfers and promotions, and deprovisioning accounts promptly at termination. Gaps in lifecycle management — particularly delayed or incomplete deprovisioning — leave orphaned accounts that adversaries actively target for initial access and privilege escalation during intrusion campaigns against organizations of every size and sector.

Authentication Systems

The mechanisms by which users prove their identity before being granted access to any protected resource. Modern IAM platforms support multiple authentication methods — from passwords and MFA tokens to biometrics and certificate-based authentication — and adapt requirements based on risk signals and contextual factors including device posture, user location, and behavioral baseline deviation from established norms. The strength of authentication methods employed is the single most impactful variable in the probability of account compromise across all attack categories that begin with credential theft or phishing.

Authorization Policies and Access Control

The rules that define what an authenticated identity is permitted to access and what actions they may perform on those resources. Policies may be role-based, attribute-based, or governed by fine-grained entitlements evaluated at runtime. Policy engines evaluate these rules in real-time for every access request — applying consistent enforcement logic across all enrolled applications and services regardless of their underlying technology or deployment model. Consistent policy enforcement is the mechanism that ensures an IAM system’s security guarantees hold even as the environment’s scale and complexity grow over time.

User Provisioning and Deprovisioning

Automated workflows that create, update, and remove access entitlements across all connected systems when a user’s organizational status or role changes. Automated provisioning reduces the manual errors that produce both under-provisioning — which impedes productivity — and over-provisioning — which creates unnecessary security exposure through excessive entitlements. Automated deprovisioning is operationally critical: manual processes consistently produce delays measured in days or weeks during which terminated users retain active access to sensitive systems, creating both regulatory exposure and insider threat risk simultaneously across the organization’s full application portfolio.

Access Control Enforcement

The runtime layer that intercepts access requests and enforces policy decisions before granting resource access. Enforcement can be implemented via identity-aware proxies, policy enforcement points, API gateways, or native integrations within applications and operating systems. Enforcement must be consistent across all access paths — including direct system access, API calls, and administrative interfaces — because adversaries specifically seek gaps where the IAM policy layer can be bypassed to reach protected resources without triggering the authentication and authorization controls that the IAM system provides at the primary access path into each protected system.

Authentication Methods

The strength of any IAM system depends substantially on the authentication methods it employs. Weak authentication is consistently among the leading causes of account compromise across every industry sector and organization size. Modern IAM platforms support a range of methods suited to different risk profiles, user populations, and access scenarios — and organizations that deploy the strongest available methods see measurably fewer credential-based intrusions than those relying on legacy authentication mechanisms without stronger supplementary controls.

Passwords

The oldest and most widely deployed authentication method. Users prove identity by providing a shared secret. Passwords remain pervasive but are highly vulnerable to phishing, credential stuffing, password spray, and brute-force attacks when not paired with stronger controls. Password policies requiring length, complexity, and uniqueness reduce but do not eliminate this risk — MFA enforcement is essential for any system protecting sensitive resources, regulated data, or privileged administrative functions.

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

Requires users to present two or more verification factors from distinct categories: something you know (password), something you have (hardware token, authenticator app), or something you are (biometric). MFA is one of the highest-impact controls available in enterprise security — it blocks the vast majority of credential-based account compromise attempts even when passwords are fully known to the attacker through prior data breach exposure, phishing, or network interception of authentication traffic.

Biometric Authentication

Verifies identity using unique physical or behavioral characteristics — fingerprints, facial geometry, iris patterns, or voice. Biometrics provide strong assurance and frictionless user experience but require secure enrollment processes, privacy-compliant data handling, and robust fallback mechanisms. Increasingly standard on mobile devices and modern workstations as the primary local authentication method, typically paired with cloud identity verification for resource access decisions that require network-level policy evaluation.

Single Sign-On (SSO)

Allows users to authenticate once to a centralized identity provider and then access multiple connected applications without re-authenticating to each individually. SSO improves both user experience and security posture simultaneously — fewer repeated logins means fewer credential exposure opportunities, and centralized authentication enables consistent MFA enforcement and session monitoring across all enrolled applications. SAML, OAuth 2.0, and OpenID Connect are the dominant SSO protocols deployed across enterprise IAM architectures globally.

Adaptive Authentication

Adjusts authentication requirements in real time based on risk signals: user location, device health posture, IP reputation, time of access, and behavioral baseline deviation. Low-risk sessions from trusted devices may proceed with a password alone; anomalous sessions trigger step-up authentication challenges requiring additional verification. Adaptive approaches balance security with usability by applying friction proportional to the actual risk level of each specific access attempt rather than imposing maximum friction uniformly across all user sessions.

Passwordless Authentication

Eliminates the password entirely in favor of phishing-resistant alternatives — hardware security keys (FIDO2/WebAuthn), passkeys, or biometric-only flows that bind authentication to a specific device and user. Passwordless authentication removes the credential theft attack surface entirely for enrolled systems. Adoption is accelerating as passkey support matures across browsers, operating systems, and identity provider platforms and the user experience friction of earlier passwordless approaches has been substantially reduced for mainstream enterprise deployment.

Access Control Models

Once identity is verified, authorization policies determine what that identity may access and what actions they may perform on those resources. Different organizations adopt different access control models depending on the complexity of their environments, regulatory requirements, and operational workflows. Understanding the tradeoffs between models is essential for security architects designing IAM programs that are both secure and administratively sustainable at enterprise scale over the long operational horizon of a mature program.

RBAC

Role-Based Access Control

Assigns permissions to roles, and roles to users. Users inherit all permissions associated with their assigned roles. RBAC is the most widely deployed model — intuitive to administer and naturally aligned with organizational job functions. Requires careful role design and active governance to prevent role sprawl and privilege accumulation over organizational transitions that add roles without removing outdated ones from user assignments.

ABAC

Attribute-Based Access Control

Makes access decisions based on attributes of the user, resource, action, and environment simultaneously. ABAC provides fine-grained, context-sensitive control that RBAC cannot achieve alone — well-suited to complex environments where access depends on multiple conditions such as data classification level, user security clearance, department, and current operating location or session context.

PBAC

Policy-Based Access Control

Centralizes access control logic into explicit, auditable policies managed by a dedicated policy engine. Access decisions are computed by evaluating policy rules against the current request context at runtime. Enables consistent enforcement across heterogeneous environments and supports the compliance reporting requirements of frameworks mandating documented, verifiable, and independently auditable access control logic for all in-scope regulated systems.

Least Privilege

Least Privilege Access

A foundational security principle stating that users and systems should receive the minimum access necessary to perform their function and nothing more. Applying least privilege limits the blast radius of a compromised account. It is a design goal that all access control models should implement — not a standalone mechanism — and requires active ongoing governance through periodic access reviews to maintain as organizational roles and responsibilities evolve continuously over time.

Most mature IAM programs combine multiple models rather than relying exclusively on one approach. RBAC provides the foundational access structure aligned with job functions; ABAC adds context-sensitive enforcement for high-sensitivity resources where simple role membership is insufficient to make a correct access decision; least privilege is applied as a design constraint across all role and policy definitions. The combination produces access control that is both administratively manageable and appropriately fine-grained for the diverse access scenarios the organization must govern securely and consistently across its full application and infrastructure portfolio.

How IAM Works

When a user or system attempts to access a protected resource, the IAM platform orchestrates a structured decision process that occurs in near-real time. Understanding each stage of this process helps security architects design access control systems that are robust, auditable, and explainable during incident investigations, compliance audits, and access review exercises that require clear documentation of how access decisions are made and recorded for each user population.

Stage 1 — Access Request Initiated

The user or system initiates an access request — launching an application, calling an API, connecting to a remote system, or querying a database resource. The request is intercepted by an identity-aware enforcement point before reaching the target resource. This enforcement point may be an identity-aware proxy, an API gateway, a cloud access security broker, or a native application integration depending on the architecture of the specific access path being protected by the IAM platform.

Stage 2 — Identity Claim Presented

The user presents identity claims: a username, certificate, token, or other identifier. The IAM system checks these claims against authoritative identity stores such as Active Directory, an LDAP directory, or a cloud identity provider. The identity store is the authoritative source of record for what identities exist within the organization and what attributes are associated with each — including role assignments, group memberships, and account status that feeds directly into the authorization decision that follows successful authentication.

Stage 3 — Authentication Check

The IAM system evaluates whether the presented credentials satisfy the required authentication assurance level for the requested resource. Adaptive controls may require additional factors based on risk signals including device posture, geographic location, or behavioral anomalies inconsistent with the user’s established baseline. If authentication fails, the session is denied immediately and an audit log entry is generated. Repeated failures may trigger account lockout or security alert generation depending on configured policy thresholds and the sensitivity of the targeted resource.

Stage 4 — Policy Evaluation

The policy engine retrieves the authenticated user’s roles, attributes, and group memberships and evaluates them against the access control policies governing the requested resource. Context — time of access, geographic location, device compliance status, data classification of the target resource — is incorporated where applicable based on the access control model in use. This evaluation is the core intelligence layer of the IAM system: where identity information is translated into a concrete, enforceable access decision for the specific request being processed at that moment.

Stage 5 — Authorization Decision

The policy engine issues a decision: permit, deny, or conditional access pending additional verification such as step-up MFA or manager approval through a workflow integration. This decision is logged for audit purposes, providing a reliable, tamper-evident record of every access determination made by the system across all users and resources over time. The audit trail is essential for both security investigations requiring reconstruction of access patterns and compliance evidence collection under regulatory frameworks requiring documented access control decisions for in-scope systems and data categories.

Stage 6 — Access Granted or Denied

The enforcement point acts on the policy decision. Permitted sessions receive a scoped access token or session credential that grants access to authorized resources and only those resources — the scope of the token is explicitly bounded by the authorization decision and cannot be extended without a new authorization evaluation. Denied sessions receive an appropriate error response. All outcomes — both grants and denials — are logged and available to security operations for monitoring, threat hunting, and compliance review by authorized personnel with access to the IAM audit infrastructure.

IAM vs. PAM vs. Zero Trust

IAM is frequently discussed alongside related security disciplines — particularly Privileged Access Management (PAM) and Zero Trust architecture. Understanding how these disciplines differ and complement each other is essential for architects building comprehensive identity security programs. They are not competing alternatives or redundant investments; they are layered capabilities that each address distinct dimensions of the identity security problem space that the others are not designed to handle independently.

Dimension IAM PAM Zero Trust
Primary Purpose Govern all digital identities and their access rights across the enterprise environment and application portfolio Secure and control access to privileged accounts, administrative credentials, and high-risk systems Eliminate implicit trust by continuously verifying every access request regardless of network location or prior authentication
Scope All users: employees, contractors, partners, service accounts, and machine identities across all systems Privileged users only: domain admins, root accounts, service accounts with elevated rights to critical systems All entities: users, devices, workloads, services, and all network traffic flows across every access path
Security Focus Authentication strength, authorization enforcement, and identity lifecycle governance across all enrolled systems Credential vaulting, session recording, just-in-time elevation, and privileged session monitoring and auditing Continuous verification, microsegmentation, least privilege enforcement, and an organizational assume-breach posture
Typical Use Cases SSO, MFA enforcement, automated user provisioning, access reviews, and compliance evidence collection Securing domain admin accounts, vaulting service account passwords, auditing privileged administrative sessions Cloud-native security architecture, eliminating VPN dependency, and securing remote and hybrid workforce access
Relationship Foundation — provides the identity fabric that PAM and Zero Trust architecturally depend upon Extends IAM with controls specific to the highest-risk privileged access scenarios in the environment Architectural model that depends on strong IAM as its primary enabling capability for continuous verification

In practice, mature organizations deploy all three layers in an integrated architecture rather than treating them as alternative approaches to the same problem. IAM provides the identity foundation; PAM hardens the highest-risk privileged subset of that identity population with controls that standard IAM is not designed to provide; Zero Trust applies the overarching principle of continuous verification across the entire access surface. Gaps in any one layer can undermine the protection provided by the others if adversaries discover and exploit the seams between them during an active intrusion campaign.

Benefits of IAM

Organizations that invest in mature IAM programs experience measurable improvements across security, operations, and compliance posture. The benefits compound over time as identity governance matures, automation reduces manual overhead, and the organization develops the institutional knowledge required to operate IAM controls effectively at enterprise scale across a continuously evolving application and infrastructure environment.

Reduced Credential-Based Attack Surface

MFA, adaptive authentication, and phishing-resistant credential standards significantly reduce the probability of account compromise from credential theft, phishing, and brute-force attacks — the most common initial access vectors across all enterprise breach categories regardless of industry or organization size. Organizations with mandatory MFA enforcement experience dramatically lower account takeover rates than those relying on passwords alone, regardless of how frequently their users encounter credential-targeting attack campaigns across their normal operating environment and communications channels.

Consistent, Auditable Access Control

Centralized policy enforcement ensures that access decisions are consistent, auditable, and aligned with organizational security requirements across every enrolled application and service. Without IAM, access control logic is scattered across individual systems — each with its own policy configuration, audit trail, and governance process that no single team has visibility into comprehensively. Centralization makes the overall access posture visible and governable in a way that distributed, system-by-system access management structurally cannot achieve regardless of how carefully each individual system is independently configured and maintained.

Reduced Insider Threat Risk

By enforcing minimum necessary access and maintaining comprehensive access logs, IAM systems constrain what malicious or negligent insiders can reach and provide the audit trail needed to detect and investigate anomalous activity after it occurs. Least privilege enforcement means that a compromised or abused account can only access the resources that role requires — containing the blast radius of any single identity compromise to a defined and bounded subset of organizational data and systems rather than allowing lateral movement across the full scope of what an over-privileged account could otherwise reach during an insider incident or account takeover scenario.

Simplified User Management at Scale

Automated provisioning and deprovisioning reduce the administrative burden associated with managing access across dozens or hundreds of applications for large user populations. Role changes propagate automatically when HR systems trigger lifecycle events, reducing the manual processing time and error rate that characterize ticket-based access management approaches. Organizations with hundreds or thousands of employees and contractors simply cannot sustain accurate access management through manual processes at scale — automation is not merely operationally convenient but is architecturally necessary for maintaining a defensible access posture across a large and continuously changing identity population.

Regulatory Compliance Support

Frameworks including SOC 2, ISO 27001, CMMC, HIPAA, and PCI-DSS all include access control requirements that a mature IAM program directly satisfies and provides evidence for. Centralized IAM governance simplifies compliance evidence collection by providing a single system of record for access entitlements, authentication events, and policy enforcement history across all in-scope systems. The access review and recertification workflows built into mature IAM programs directly produce the attestation evidence that auditors require to validate that the organization maintains documented, reviewed, and actively governed access controls aligned with the requirements of each applicable compliance framework.

Challenges of IAM

Deploying and sustaining a mature IAM program is a substantial organizational undertaking that extends well beyond initial platform procurement and deployment. Security teams and security leaders should approach IAM implementation with realistic expectations about the ongoing complexity involved and invest in the processes, tooling, and personnel required to sustain the program operationally long after the initial deployment is completed and the implementation team has moved to other priorities.

Complex Identity Environments

Modern organizations operate across hybrid cloud, multi-cloud, and on-premises environments with hundreds of applications maintained by multiple teams using different authentication mechanisms and access control models that were never designed to interoperate consistently. Managing identity governance across this heterogeneous landscape requires careful architecture decisions, ongoing investment, and continuous remediation of the inconsistencies that accumulate when teams make independent access management decisions without centralized standards, oversight, or enforcement across the full scope of the organizational technology environment.

Legacy System Integration

Many organizations maintain critical systems — ERP platforms, industrial control systems, purpose-built applications — that lack modern authentication protocol support and cannot be directly integrated with contemporary IAM platforms without significant custom development effort. Integrating these systems requires intermediary adapters, custom connectors, or architectural workarounds that are complex to build and expensive to maintain across platform version upgrades. Legacy systems often become permanent IAM exceptions that create structural gaps in coverage that adversaries specifically target during intrusion campaigns targeting organizations in those sectors.

Identity Lifecycle Management at Scale

Managing identity lifecycle events — joiner, mover, leaver — accurately and promptly across all connected systems is operationally complex in large organizations with high employee turnover, frequent role changes, and complex contractor and partner identity populations that do not map cleanly to standard HR-driven provisioning workflows. Delays in deprovisioning or inaccurate role assignments create security exposure that persists until corrected through access reviews — reviews that are themselves time-consuming and resource-intensive to conduct comprehensively across an enterprise identity population of meaningful size and diversity.

Permission Creep and Entitlement Sprawl

Role design and entitlement management are among the most technically complex and organizationally demanding aspects of sustained IAM operations. Permission creep — the gradual accumulation of access rights beyond what a user’s current function requires — is a persistent failure mode that grows silently as roles are added during organizational transitions and never removed when they are no longer needed. Without regular access reviews, periodic recertification campaigns, and active lifecycle management governance, entitlement sprawl becomes a significant and structurally difficult-to-remediate risk that grows larger and more expensive to address with every month that systematic remediation is deferred by operational teams under competing workload pressures.

User Adoption and Authentication Friction

Security controls that significantly increase friction for legitimate users consistently face adoption resistance that, if unmanaged, produces workarounds that undermine the very controls they are meant to bypass. MFA fatigue attacks specifically exploit authentication prompt fatigue to manipulate users into approving fraudulent authentication requests. Balancing security requirements with user experience through SSO that reduces repeated authentication, adaptive controls that apply friction proportional to risk, and phishing-resistant passkeys that eliminate password burden entirely — is an ongoing design and operations challenge for IAM programs serving large and operationally demanding user populations across diverse access contexts and device types.

Key Takeaways

  • Identity and Access Management (IAM) governs the creation, authentication, authorization, and deprovisioning of all digital identities across an organization’s systems and applications — it is a standing governance function requiring continuous operational investment, not a one-time project that concludes after the initial platform deployment is completed and the implementation team moves to other priorities.
  • Identity has replaced the network perimeter as the primary security boundary in cloud-centric and hybrid environments. Controlling and monitoring identity is now more consequential to security outcomes than controlling network perimeter traffic in most modern enterprise architectures where applications and data are distributed across multiple cloud providers and SaaS platforms.
  • Authentication proves who a user is; authorization determines what that verified user is permitted to do — these are distinct processes that must both be rigorously enforced. A failure in either creates exploitable security exposure regardless of how strong the other control is configured and operating within the IAM platform at the time of the compromise.
  • Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) is one of the highest-impact security controls available in enterprise environments and blocks the vast majority of credential-based account compromise attempts even when passwords are fully known to the attacker through prior data breach exposure, phishing, or credential theft from a compromised endpoint or intercepted network session.
  • Access control models — including RBAC, ABAC, and policy-based approaches — each carry distinct operational tradeoffs. The least privilege principle should govern all access design decisions across every model and requires active ongoing governance through access reviews to maintain as organizational roles, responsibilities, and technology environments continuously evolve over time in ways that create entitlement drift from the intended security posture.
  • IAM, PAM, and Zero Trust are complementary and layered security disciplines that address distinct dimensions of the identity security problem. IAM provides the identity foundation; PAM secures the privileged access subset; Zero Trust provides the architectural model of continuous verification. Gaps in any one layer can undermine the protection provided by the others during a targeted intrusion campaign that probes for seams in the layered defense architecture.
  • Identity lifecycle management — particularly timely and accurate deprovisioning at employee termination and contractor offboarding — is a critical and consistently underinvested operational discipline that directly impacts security posture by eliminating the orphaned accounts that adversaries specifically target as low-effort, low-detection initial access vectors into organizational environments of every size and sector.
  • Permission creep and entitlement sprawl are persistent IAM failure modes that grow silently over time without active governance investment. Regular access reviews and formal recertification campaigns are operationally necessary to detect and remediate accumulated excessive entitlements before they are discovered and exploited by adversaries conducting reconnaissance against identity attack surfaces within the organization’s environment.
  • Mature IAM programs directly support compliance with SOC 2, ISO 27001, CMMC, PCI-DSS, HIPAA, and other regulatory frameworks that require documented access control evidence, access review records, and audit trails demonstrating that systems are accessed only by authorized users with current, appropriate entitlements that have been reviewed and attested within required timeframes by accountable organizational stakeholders.
  • Passwordless authentication technologies — FIDO2 hardware security keys, passkeys, and biometric-only authentication flows — represent the current direction of identity security evolution by eliminating the credential theft attack surface entirely for enrolled systems, rendering the most frequently exploited initial access vector operationally irrelevant for organizations that deploy these controls comprehensively across their user population and application portfolio.
