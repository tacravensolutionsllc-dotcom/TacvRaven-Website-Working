What is SOC 2?

Understanding SOC 2 compliance, the five Trust Services Criteria, and why SOC 2 audits are essential for organizations that store, process, or manage customer data.

In an era where data breaches dominate headlines and third-party risk is under constant regulatory scrutiny, organizations that handle customer information face mounting pressure to prove that their security practices are more than a promise on paper. SOC 2 — the Service Organization Control 2 framework — has emerged as the gold standard for independently validating how service providers design, implement, and operate security controls. Whether you are a cloud provider, a SaaS company, or a managed security services firm, a SOC 2 report signals to customers and partners that your security posture has been assessed against a rigorous, standardized set of criteria by an independent, licensed auditor.

Executive Overview

SOC 2, or Service Organization Control 2, is an auditing framework developed by the American Institute of Certified Public Accountants (AICPA). It defines criteria for evaluating how service organizations manage and protect customer data across five Trust Services Criteria (TSC): Security, Availability, Processing Integrity, Confidentiality, and Privacy. The framework was designed specifically for businesses that provide services to other organizations and, in doing so, store, process, or transmit sensitive customer data on their behalf.

The AICPA established the SOC reporting framework to address the growing demand for transparent, verifiable assurance over the controls operated by third-party service providers. Unlike regulatory mandates such as HIPAA or PCI DSS, SOC 2 is not a government regulation — it is a voluntary attestation standard. However, it has become effectively mandatory in many industries and procurement processes. Enterprise customers routinely require SOC 2 reports from vendors before signing contracts that involve access to sensitive data or critical systems.

At its core, SOC 2 evaluates whether a service organization's controls are suitably designed and, in the case of a Type II examination, operating effectively over time to meet the Trust Services Criteria relevant to its operations. An independent, licensed CPA firm conducts the audit and issues the report — providing customers and stakeholders with objective, third-party evidence that the organization's security controls meet the applicable standards.

SOC 2 is not prescriptive about which specific technologies or tools an organization must use. It establishes principles and criteria that organizations must satisfy, allowing them to select controls and processes appropriate to their environment and risk profile. This flexibility makes SOC 2 applicable across a wide range of service organizations — from cloud infrastructure providers to payroll software platforms to managed detection and response firms.

Understanding the SOC 2 Framework

The AICPA's System and Organization Controls (SOC) suite includes three primary report types, each designed for a different purpose and audience. Understanding how these reports differ is foundational to understanding why SOC 2 holds its current position as the dominant assurance standard for technology service providers.

SOC 1 — Internal Controls Over Financial Reporting

SOC 1 reports focus on a service organization's internal controls over financial reporting (ICFR). These reports are relevant when the services provided may affect the financial statements of the user organization — for example, payroll processors, benefit plan administrators, or transaction clearing houses. SOC 1 reports are governed by SSAE No. 18 and are primarily used by financial auditors and senior management evaluating third-party financial risk.

SOC 2 — Security, Availability, and Data Protection

SOC 2 reports focus on a service organization's controls relevant to the Trust Services Criteria. They are intended for a broader audience — business partners, customers, procurement teams, regulators, and security professionals — who need assurance that the service provider is managing customer data responsibly. SOC 2 is the most operationally relevant report type for technology and cloud service providers.

SOC 3 — General Use Summary Report

SOC 3 reports are publicly distributable, summarized versions of SOC 2 reports. They convey a high-level conclusion that the organization's controls meet the applicable Trust Services Criteria, without disclosing the detailed testing procedures and results contained in a SOC 2. SOC 3 reports are commonly published on an organization's website as a marketing-facing trust signal. They do not replace SOC 2 for enterprise procurement purposes.

For organizations handling sensitive customer information — cloud providers, SaaS companies, data processors, managed service providers, and IT outsourcing firms — SOC 2 is the most widely requested and operationally significant report type. Enterprise customers and regulated-industry procurement teams require SOC 2 for the depth of assurance it provides before entrusting their data to a third party.

The Five Trust Services Criteria

The Trust Services Criteria (TSC) form the evaluative foundation of every SOC 2 engagement. Each criterion addresses a distinct dimension of how a service organization protects and manages customer data. The Security criterion — also called the Common Criteria — is required in every SOC 2 audit. The remaining four criteria are included based on the nature of the services provided and the commitments made to customers in service agreements.

01 — Security

The foundational, mandatory criterion. Evaluates whether the organization's systems are protected against unauthorized access, unauthorized disclosure of information, and damage to systems that could compromise the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of data. Controls evaluated include access management policies, multi-factor authentication, vulnerability management, network monitoring, encryption, and security incident response. Every SOC 2 audit must include the Security criterion.

02 — Availability

Evaluates whether systems are available for operation and use as committed or agreed upon in customer contracts and service level agreements. Organizations must demonstrate controls related to system performance monitoring, capacity planning, disaster recovery, business continuity management, and incident response procedures that restore services within agreed timeframes. This criterion is particularly relevant for cloud infrastructure and SaaS providers where uptime commitments are contractually defined.

03 — Processing Integrity

Assesses whether system processing is complete, valid, accurate, timely, and authorized. This criterion is especially relevant for organizations processing financial transactions, healthcare claims, or other critical business data on behalf of customers where errors or incomplete processing could have material consequences. Controls evaluated include data validation routines, error detection and correction procedures, transaction completeness checks, and exception reporting processes.

04 — Confidentiality

Evaluates whether information designated as confidential is protected as committed or agreed. Organizations must demonstrate controls for identifying and classifying confidential data, restricting access to authorized personnel, protecting data in transit and at rest through encryption, and appropriately disposing of confidential information at the conclusion of its retention period or contractual lifecycle. This criterion addresses how organizations protect customer data designated as confidential under contractual terms.

05 — Privacy

Assesses whether personal information is collected, used, retained, disclosed, and disposed of in conformity with the organization's privacy notice and with criteria established in the AICPA's Generally Accepted Privacy Principles (GAPP). This criterion addresses consent management, purpose limitation, data subject rights, and privacy program governance. Organizations subject to GDPR, CCPA, or other privacy regulations frequently include this criterion to demonstrate alignment between their privacy program and customer commitments.

SOC 2 Type I vs. Type II

SOC 2 examinations are issued in two forms — Type I and Type II — that differ significantly in scope, time horizon, and the level of assurance they provide. Understanding the distinction is essential for organizations preparing for an audit and for customers evaluating what a vendor's SOC 2 report actually represents.

SOC 2 Type II reports provide substantially stronger assurance because they demonstrate that controls are not only appropriately designed but have been consistently operating as intended over an extended observation period. Enterprise clients and security-conscious procurement teams routinely require Type II reports before approving vendors for systems that handle sensitive data. Organizations should treat Type I as a valuable stepping stone — demonstrating initial commitment — while prioritizing Type II as their sustained compliance target.

How a SOC 2 Audit Works

A SOC 2 audit follows a structured lifecycle moving from initial control design through independent verification and formal report issuance. Each phase builds on the previous, culminating in an auditor's opinion on whether the organization's controls meet the applicable Trust Services Criteria. The process typically spans six to twelve months for a Type II engagement.

Phase 1 — Security Control Design

The organization designs and implements security controls aligned to the selected Trust Services Criteria. This phase begins with a risk assessment to identify threats and control gaps, followed by selection and implementation of appropriate controls. The organization must formally define the scope of the system description — the boundaries of people, processes, and technology that will be covered in the audit. Readiness assessments conducted by the auditing firm or a third-party advisor are commonly used at this stage to identify gaps before the formal observation period begins.

Phase 2 — Control Documentation

Controls are formally documented in policies, procedures, and the system description that will appear in the SOC 2 report. Documentation must include information security policies, access control procedures, change management processes, incident response plans, business continuity plans, and vendor management procedures. All documentation must be current, formally approved by management, and accessible to personnel responsible for operating the controls. Missing, outdated, or informal documentation is among the most common sources of audit findings.

Phase 3 — Independent Auditor Review

A licensed CPA firm with SOC attestation experience is engaged to conduct the examination. The auditor reviews the organization's system description, examines documented policies and procedures, evaluates the design of controls, and — for Type II engagements — begins the formal observation period during which controls must operate consistently. The auditor issues planning documents specifying which controls will be tested and what evidence will be required.

Phase 4 — Testing of Controls

The auditor conducts testing to evaluate whether controls operated effectively throughout the review period. Testing methods include inquiry (structured interviews with personnel responsible for controls), observation (watching processes in execution), inspection (reviewing documentation, tickets, logs, and records), and re-performance (independently executing a control procedure to verify that results match expected outcomes). Evidence is collected, indexed, and evaluated against the Trust Services Criteria for each control tested.

Phase 5 — Audit Report Issuance

The CPA firm issues the SOC 2 report containing the auditor's opinion, management's assertion about the system description and controls, the detailed description of the system, and — for Type II — the testing procedures and results for each control. The report may contain an unqualified opinion (indicating controls met criteria) or may identify exceptions where controls did not operate as designed. Organizations address exceptions in subsequent audit cycles and may provide a management response within the current report.

Common SOC 2 Security Controls

While SOC 2 does not mandate specific technologies, certain categories of controls appear consistently across virtually all SOC 2 audits because they directly address the requirements of the Trust Services Criteria. These controls form the operational backbone of a defensible security program and are evaluated for both design and — in Type II engagements — consistent operational effectiveness.

Access Control Policies

Formal policies governing who can access systems, applications, and data — including role-based access controls, least privilege enforcement, periodic access recertification reviews, and access termination procedures for departing personnel. Auditors evaluate whether access is appropriately restricted and consistently reviewed on a defined schedule.

Identity & Authentication

Controls ensuring that only authenticated and authorized users can access systems and sensitive data. Commonly evaluated controls include multi-factor authentication (MFA), password policy enforcement, privileged access management (PAM), and single sign-on (SSO) configurations with appropriate session timeout and re-authentication requirements.

Encryption & Data Protection

Technical controls protecting customer data at rest and in transit. Auditors evaluate encryption standards applied to databases, storage volumes, and network transmissions — including TLS version requirements, key management practices, and data classification policies that govern what data requires encryption and under what conditions.

Monitoring & Logging

Controls providing continuous visibility into system and user activity. Auditors evaluate whether security events are captured in logs, whether log integrity is maintained and protected from tampering, whether alerts are configured for anomalous or unauthorized activity, and whether security monitoring outputs are reviewed consistently by qualified personnel.

Incident Response

Formal processes for detecting, reporting, classifying, containing, eradicating, and recovering from security incidents. Auditors evaluate whether a documented incident response plan exists, whether it has been tested through tabletop exercises or simulations, and whether past incidents were tracked, escalated, and resolved within defined timeframes with appropriate documentation.

Vendor Risk Management

Processes for evaluating and monitoring the security posture of third-party vendors and subservice organizations with access to customer data or systems. Controls include pre-engagement security assessments, contractual security and data protection requirements, ongoing vendor monitoring, and periodic reassessment of subservice organization risk against defined criteria.

Who Needs SOC 2 Compliance

SOC 2 compliance is increasingly treated as a baseline market expectation rather than a differentiator. While it is not a statutory mandate in the way that HIPAA or GDPR are enforced by regulators, the practical reality for service organizations is that enterprise customers, regulated industries, and security-conscious procurement teams routinely require current SOC 2 reports before awarding contracts or allowing access to sensitive systems and data.

  • Cloud Service Providers Organizations delivering IaaS, PaaS, or SaaS hosted in cloud environments. Enterprise clients and regulated industries require CSPs to demonstrate SOC 2 compliance as part of vendor onboarding and ongoing third-party risk management programs.
  • SaaS Companies SaaS vendors processing customer business data — HR records, financial data, CRM data, or healthcare information — face strong market demand for SOC 2 Type II reports from enterprise clients who must demonstrate third-party security assurance to their own stakeholders and regulators.
  • Managed Service Providers MSPs and MSSPs providing managed IT, security operations, or infrastructure services to client organizations. MSPs typically hold broad access to client environments and data, making SOC 2 a critical assurance mechanism for their enterprise client base.
  • Data Processors Organizations processing large volumes of customer or consumer data on behalf of other businesses — analytics platforms, data enrichment services, and data warehousing providers — must demonstrate robust data protection practices to clients and their own downstream regulators.
  • Healthcare Technology Vendors Technology companies in the healthcare sector frequently pursue SOC 2 alongside HIPAA compliance to demonstrate comprehensive security governance. Healthcare organization clients often require SOC 2 to satisfy their own vendor management and risk assessment obligations.
  • Financial Technology Providers FinTech companies and payment processors face heightened scrutiny from financial institution clients. SOC 2 reports complement PCI DSS compliance and provide broader security assurance covering controls that PCI DSS does not address.

SOC 2 has become a standard market expectation because it delivers verified, auditor-attested evidence of security program maturity — something that self-assessments, security questionnaires, and vendor certifications cannot credibly provide. For organizations selling services to enterprises, government contractors, or regulated industries, SOC 2 compliance has effectively become a prerequisite for doing business at scale.

Benefits of SOC 2 Compliance

The value of SOC 2 compliance extends well beyond satisfying a vendor questionnaire. Organizations that achieve and maintain SOC 2 compliance consistently realize tangible benefits across customer relationships, internal security maturity, competitive positioning, and operational risk management.

Improved Customer Trust

A SOC 2 report provides customers with independent, auditor-attested evidence that the organization's security controls are designed and operating effectively. This third-party validation is far more credible than self-assessments or vendor-completed security questionnaires and significantly strengthens trust in vendor relationships — particularly in enterprise sales cycles where security review is a formal gate.

Demonstrated Security Maturity

Preparing for a SOC 2 audit requires organizations to formalize policies, document procedures, and implement consistent controls across their environment. This discipline produces a more mature, defensible security program — not merely a report to distribute to customers. Many organizations discover and remediate significant security gaps during SOC 2 readiness preparation that would otherwise have remained unaddressed.

Competitive Advantage

In competitive markets where multiple vendors offer comparable technical capabilities, a current SOC 2 Type II report can be a decisive factor in enterprise procurement decisions. Organizations with active SOC 2 reports typically move through customer security reviews faster than competitors without one — reducing sales cycle friction and accelerating revenue recognition.

Stronger Internal Security Practices

SOC 2 requirements drive organizations to implement controls — periodic access reviews, incident response plan testing, vulnerability management programs, change control procedures — that improve actual security outcomes independent of compliance posture. The audit process surfaces operational gaps that internal teams may have normalized or deprioritized.

Improved Risk Management

SOC 2 preparation requires organizations to conduct formal risk assessments, evaluate subservice organization risk, and continuously monitor their control environment. These activities produce structured, ongoing risk visibility that supports better security investment decisions and demonstrates risk management discipline to customers, investors, and insurers.

Challenges of SOC 2 Compliance

While the benefits of SOC 2 compliance are substantial, organizations should approach the process with a realistic understanding of the significant effort, investment, and ongoing commitment required to achieve and maintain a clean SOC 2 report. Underestimating this commitment is the most common cause of failed first-year audits and missed customer deadlines.

Implementation Complexity

SOC 2 compliance requires designing and implementing controls across multiple domains simultaneously — access management, security monitoring, incident response, change management, business continuity, and vendor risk. For organizations without a formal security program, building these controls from scratch requires substantial time, technical expertise, and organizational coordination. The Common Criteria alone address dozens of specific control requirements that must be met before an audit observation period can begin.

Documentation Requirements

SOC 2 auditors require extensive, contemporaneous evidence of control operation. Organizations must maintain formal policies, procedures, and records demonstrating that controls were consistently applied throughout the review period. All policies must be current, management-approved, distributed to relevant personnel, and evidenced as acknowledged. Missing, outdated, or informal documentation — including policies that exist but have not been reviewed within the required timeframe — is among the most common sources of audit exceptions in first-year engagements.

Ongoing Monitoring and Maintenance

SOC 2 compliance is not a one-time achievement — it is a continuous program. Controls must operate consistently throughout each annual audit period, and organizations must maintain their control environment between audits without degradation. This requires ongoing access reviews, security monitoring, vulnerability scanning, patch management, change control compliance, and periodic testing of incident response and disaster recovery capabilities. Each of these activities requires dedicated personnel time and management commitment sustained across the full year.

Audit Preparation Costs

The total cost of a SOC 2 program varies significantly based on organizational size, scope complexity, and existing security maturity. Organizations should anticipate meaningful investment across readiness assessments, remediation activities, compliance tooling, auditor fees, and internal personnel time. For growing organizations pursuing SOC 2 for the first time, total first-year costs — including both preparation and audit fees — commonly range from tens of thousands to hundreds of thousands of dollars depending on scope, organizational size, and the extent of remediation required before the observation period begins.

Key Takeaways

  • SOC 2 is an auditing framework developed by the AICPA for evaluating how service organizations manage and protect customer data. It is not a government regulation — it is a voluntary attestation standard that has become a de facto market requirement for technology and service providers.
  • The five Trust Services Criteria are Security (required in all SOC 2 audits), Availability, Processing Integrity, Confidentiality, and Privacy. Organizations select criteria relevant to their services and the commitments made to customers in service agreements.
  • SOC 2 Type I reports evaluate control design at a single point in time. SOC 2 Type II reports evaluate both design suitability and operational effectiveness over an observation period of at least six months — providing substantially stronger assurance that enterprise customers require.
  • An independent, licensed CPA firm conducts SOC 2 examinations. The auditor's opinion provides third-party validation that organizations cannot achieve through self-assessment or vendor questionnaires alone.
  • The SOC 2 audit lifecycle progresses through control design, documentation, auditor review, control testing, and formal report issuance. For a Type II engagement, the observation period typically spans six to twelve months.
  • Common controls evaluated in SOC 2 audits include access management policies, multi-factor authentication, encryption, security monitoring and logging, incident response procedures, and vendor risk management processes.
  • Cloud service providers, SaaS companies, managed service providers, data processors, and healthcare and financial technology vendors are the most common categories of organizations required to demonstrate SOC 2 compliance by enterprise customers.
  • SOC 2 compliance improves customer trust, accelerates enterprise sales cycles, strengthens internal security practices, and supports broader risk management programs — delivering security outcomes that extend well beyond the audit report itself.
  • Key challenges include implementation complexity across multiple control domains, extensive documentation requirements, the need for sustained continuous monitoring, and meaningful first-year investment in readiness, remediation, and audit fees.
  • Organizations should approach SOC 2 not as a compliance checkbox but as a discipline that produces measurable improvements in security posture, operational consistency, and organizational resilience — with the audit report as the independently verified evidence of that maturity.
